# Customer Discovery Interview Scripts — Material Bank India

**Purpose:** Validate demand before building further. The only question these scripts exist to answer: *do design/procurement professionals in India have a painful, recurring, currently-unsolved problem finding/specifying/pricing materials — and is it painful enough that they've already spent money, time, or workaround-effort on it?* We are not testing our product. We are not pitching. We are reconstructing real recent behaviour.

**Owner:** run these yourself in the first round — founder-led discovery surfaces things a hired researcher will smooth over. Target 5 interviews per segment before drawing conclusions (25 total minimum).

---

## 0. Global interviewer discipline (read before every call, every time)

This is the operating discipline from *The Mom Test*, applied to this specific market. Violating it invalidates the interview.

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** Never say "we're building a platform that..." until the interview is over (or never, unless they explicitly ask what you do). If asked early, deflect once — *"I'll explain at the end, right now I just want to understand how you work"* — and redirect.
2. **Ask about the past, never the future or hypothetical.** Ban list: "Would you use...", "Do you think you'd pay for...", "How much would you pay for...", "Would it help if...". Replace every one with: "Tell me about the last time...", "Walk me through what happened when...", "What did you do instead?"
3. **Specifics over generalities.** If they answer in generalities ("we usually just check with a few dealers"), pull them back to one concrete instance: *"Can you think of a specific project — the last one — where that happened? What was the material, which dealers, what happened next?"*
4. **Follow the money and the calendar.** "Who paid for that?" "Who signed off?" "How long did that take?" "What did it cost you — in money or in the day you lost?" These are the questions that separate a real problem from a polite complaint.
5. **Compliments are noise. Commitments and money already spent are signal.** "That's a great idea" = discard. "I built my own spreadsheet for this" or "I pay ₹X/month for Y tool" = signal.
6. **Don't pitch, don't lead, don't defend your idea if they push back on something you mention by accident.** Curiosity, not persuasion.
7. **The commission question is oblique by design (see §7 in each script).** Never ask "do you take a commission/kickback/PC from dealers" — this is a real, normalized, legally-grey practice in Indian design/architecture and a direct question gets either a lie or a shutdown. Instead ask about **process**: who calls the dealer, who negotiates, who the invoice is addressed to, how they describe their fee structure to clients. The camp reveals itself in the *mechanics* of the answer, not a confession.
8. **Recording:** ask permission at the start ("mind if I take notes / record so I don't misquote you later") — don't skip this.
9. **Silence is a tool.** After a good question, stop talking. Let them fill the space — the second sentence is usually more honest than the first.
10. **Write down verbatim phrases**, not paraphrases. "Package pricing" vs "trade discount" vs "I quote them the landed cost" are different camps — the exact words matter for the signal sheet in §6.

## 1. Interior Design Studios

### Screening criteria
Recruit only if **all** of:
- Runs or works at a studio doing residential and/or commercial interior fit-outs (not just decor/styling) with ≥3 projects/year.
- Has personally specified materials (tiles, laminates, sanitaryware, lighting, surfaces) — not purely a 3D visualizer/junior who never talks to vendors.
- Has closed or is mid-execution on a project in the **last 3 months** (recency — we need a fresh memory to interrogate, not a reconstructed one).
- Mix of studio sizes across your 5 interviews: at least one solo/2-person studio, one 5–15 person studio, one larger commercial-fitout firm.

Recruiting line (for outreach, not the interview): *"I'm researching how design studios actually source and price materials day to day — not selling anything, just trying to understand the real workflow. Could I get 25 minutes on your last project?"*

### Warm-up (2–3 min, not analyzed for signal — just gets them talking)
- "Tell me about the studio — how many people, what kind of projects, roughly how many live right now?"
- "How long have you been doing this?"

### Core questions (intent noted per question)

1. **"Walk me through your last project, start to finish, from the moment you needed to finalize a material — say, the flooring or the laminate for the kitchen — to the moment it was installed."**
   *Intent: get one full concrete narrative to anchor the rest of the interview in; surfaces the real end-to-end workflow instead of an idealized one.*

2. **"For that project, where did you first go looking for options — before you called anyone?"**
   *Intent: surfaces discovery channel — Pinterest, Instagram, WhatsApp groups, brand showrooms, dealer catalogs, MaterialDepot, Google. This is the "top of funnel" reality check.*

3. **"Once you had a shortlist, how did you actually get the price and availability? Who did you call or message, and how long did it take to hear back?"**
   *Intent: quantifies the pain in the current price-discovery loop — hours/days lost, WhatsApp back-and-forth, dealer visits. Look for a number (hours, days, number of calls).*

4. **"Did you compare that material or price against anything else — another dealer, another brand, an online listing? Tell me what you actually did."**
   *Intent: tests whether cross-source comparison happens today at all, and how (if at all) — this is the single most load-bearing question for our "price comparison" wedge.*

5. **"Was there a moment on that project where a spec you wanted turned out to be wrong or unavailable — wrong size, discontinued, wrong price — after you'd already told the client or the site team? What happened?"**
   *Intent: surfaces the cost of bad/stale data — rework, client friction, wasted site visits. This is the strongest pain signal if it exists.*

6. **"Show me — if you have it open — where you keep track of the materials and prices for a live project. Is it a spreadsheet, WhatsApp, a folder of PDFs, something else?"**
   *Intent: the artifact reveals the real system of record. A janky spreadsheet or 40 WhatsApp threads is a much stronger signal than a verbal complaint.*

7. **"When did you last visit a dealer showroom in person just to check a price or see a sample? What made that necessary — could you not get that answer any other way?"**
   *Intent: tests whether physical/showroom visits are still mandatory, and why — reveals whether specs/samples/pricing are truly opaque online.*

8. **"Tell me about a time a client pushed back on a material choice because of price. What did you do?"**
   *Intent: surfaces how price sensitivity flows through the relationship, and hints at whether the studio has room to reveal true costs (see §7 oblique probe).*

9. **"Is there any tool, app, website, or subscription you currently pay for — or your studio pays for — specifically to make sourcing or specifying materials easier? What is it, and what do you use it for?"**
   *Intent: direct WTP-via-past-behaviour signal. AutoCAD/Revit libraries, MaterialDepot samples, IndiaMART leads packages, Canva/Pinterest Pro, etc. all count.*

10. **"Who in the studio actually controls that budget — software, subscriptions, sample orders? You, or someone else?"**
    *Intent: finds the actual buyer/decision-maker for a future paid product — solo owner vs. an ops person vs. nobody (bad sign).*

11. **"If I could watch you work for a day, what's the single most annoying, repetitive task related to materials that you wish just didn't exist?"**
    *Intent: open-ended pain surfacing without asking them to invent a solution — keep listening for the same theme across interviews (that's the wedge).*

### 7. Oblique sourcing / commission-alignment probe
Ask this cluster mid-interview, framed as ordinary process questions, never as an accusation:

- *"On that last project — once the client agreed to the material, who actually placed the order with the dealer or supplier? You, the client directly, or the contractor?"*
- *"And the invoice for that material — who was it addressed to? The client, or the studio?"*
- *"How do you usually present the cost of materials to a client — do they see the dealer's price directly, or do you give them a number that already includes your markup/fee?"*
- *"Do you have a few dealers you consistently go back to across projects? What makes you stick with them?"*

**Do not react, do not follow up with "so you mark it up?" — just note the answer verbatim and move on.** The camp is visible in *whether the client ever sees a dealer's raw price* and *who the invoice is addressed to* — see the signal sheet (§ cheat sheet below).

### Wrap
- *"This has been really useful — genuinely more useful than you'd guess. Two last things: is there anyone else — another studio, a dealer you work with, a procurement person at a developer you've worked with — who'd be good for me to talk to about this same stuff?"*
- *"Would it be okay if I followed up in a few weeks, maybe with something concrete to react to, once I've spoken to more people like you?"*
- Only now, if they ask what this is for: one sentence, no pitch-deck language. *"I'm looking at whether there's a real gap in how materials get sourced and priced in India — trying to understand the problem fully before building anything."*

## 2. Architecture Firms

### Screening criteria
Recruit only if **all** of:
- Principal, senior architect, or specification lead at a firm doing built (not just competition/concept) work — residential, commercial, or institutional.
- Has personally written or approved a **materials/finishes schedule or BOQ line** in the last 6 months.
- At least one interview with a firm that does **fee-only** billing (no material markup) and at least one that has **any** commercial involvement in material selection (turnkey, design-build, or "recommends and facilitates" vendors) — you need both camps represented.
- Mix of firm sizes: solo/boutique and a 10+ person firm with a dedicated specification writer if possible.

### Warm-up
- "What kind of projects does the firm mostly do — typology, typical project size?"
- "What's your role specifically — do you personally get involved in finish/material selection, or is that someone else on your team?"

### Core questions

1. **"Tell me about the last project where you had to finalize a finish schedule — flooring, cladding, laminates, sanitaryware. Walk me through how that actually happened."**
   *Intent: anchor narrative — surfaces whether it's spreadsheet-driven, BIM-model-driven, or ad hoc.*

2. **"Where do you usually go to find out what's actually available — new tile ranges, new laminate finishes, new sanitaryware lines — before you even get to pricing?"**
   *Intent: discovery channel — brand reps visiting the office, exhibitions (like Acetech), Pinterest/Instagram, Revit/BIM libraries, dealer catalogs.*

3. **"Once you'd specified a brand and SKU in the BOQ, what happened next — did you personally get a price, or did that go to the contractor or client to sort out?"**
   *Intent: tests where architects sit in the money flow — pure spec vs. involved in procurement. Sets up §7.*

4. **"Tell me about the last time a contractor or client came back and said 'we can't get that / it's discontinued / it's too expensive, give us an alternate.' What did you do to find a substitute, and how long did it take?"**
   *Intent: this is the real pain moment — value-engineering under time pressure. Look for the actual mechanism they used to find an equivalent SKU.*

5. **"How do you currently keep your own reference library of materials and specs — is there a system, a folder, a Pinterest board, a shared drive? Show me if you can."**
   *Intent: system-of-record check — a real artifact is a much stronger signal than "we just remember."*

6. **"Have you ever specified a material, and then found out later — after installation or during a site visit — that what was installed wasn't actually what you specified, or was a lower grade? What happened?"**
   *Intent: surfaces the trust/verification gap — a core wedge for us (provenance, spec-sheet mining).*

7. **"When you need performance data — PEI rating, water absorption, fire rating, a BIS/ISO standard number — for a spec, where do you actually get it? Tell me about the last time you needed one."**
   *Intent: tests whether spec-sheet PDFs, dealer claims, or nothing at all is the current source — directly validates our "compliance/performance data" layer.*

8. **"Do you use Revit, SketchUp, or similar for these projects? When you need a specific material's BIM object or texture, where does that come from?"**
   *Intent: tests appetite/pain around BIM-linked material data — OmniClass/Uniclass relevance, potential API/plugin wedge.*

9. **"Is there a tool or subscription — for materials, for specs, for BIM content — that the firm currently pays for? What, and who uses it?"**
   *Intent: WTP-via-past-behaviour. Also surfaces incumbent tools we're competing with (BIMobject, brand-specific Revit families, etc).*

10. **"Who at the firm actually holds the budget for something like that — software, subscriptions, reference tools? You, an office manager, the principal?"**
    *Intent: identifies the real buyer for a B2B seat/API license.*

11. **"If you could wave a wand and fix one thing about how you find or verify material information for a project, what would it be?"**
    *Intent: open pain-surfacing, cross-check against other segments for common wedge themes.*

### 7. Oblique sourcing / commission-alignment probe
- *"On a recent project, once a brand/SKU was locked in the finish schedule — did you ever get involved in the actual purchase, or is that always the client's or contractor's job?"*
- *"Do clients ever ask you directly which dealer or supplier to buy from? What do you tell them?"*
- *"How is the firm's fee structured on a typical project — percentage of project cost, fixed fee, or does it vary with material selection at all?"*
- *"Do you have relationships with particular dealers or brand reps that you've built up over the years? How did those start, and what do you get out of maintaining them — samples, faster service, something else?"*

**Listen for:** fee-as-%-of-project-cost creates a *structural* (not necessarily intentional) alignment toward higher material spend — different from a direct commission but same signal direction. A firm that says "we're pure design fee, client deals with vendors directly, we don't touch pricing" is unambiguously transparency-aligned. A firm that says "we help the client negotiate" or goes quiet on "who gets the invoice" warrants a gentle non-judgmental follow-up: *"got it — and does that ever come up as a sensitive topic with clients?"* — then drop it regardless of the answer.

### Wrap
- *"Really helpful, thank you. Is there another architect, a specification writer, or a procurement contact at a developer you've worked with who'd be worth talking to?"*
- *"Can I check back in with you in a few weeks as this develops?"*

## 3. Developer / PMC Procurement Heads

### Screening criteria
Recruit only if **all** of:
- Title/role involves procurement, purchase, or vendor management for a real-estate developer, construction company, or PMC (project management consultancy) — not a design role.
- Has run a **material rate contract, vendor empanelment, or bulk purchase process** in the last 6 months (tiles/sanitaryware/laminates/lighting at minimum — not just cement/steel/rebar).
- Covers projects at meaningful scale (≥50 units residential, or equivalent commercial sqft) — the pain and the budget only show up at scale.
- If possible, get one interview from a developer (in-house procurement) and one from a PMC/consultant (procurement-as-a-service for multiple clients) — their incentives differ.

### Warm-up
- "What's your role, and what kinds of projects are you procuring materials for right now?"
- "Roughly how many active projects or how much annual material spend are you responsible for?"

### Core questions

1. **"Walk me through the last time you ran a rate contract or vendor selection for a finish material — tiles, sanitaryware, laminates, lighting. From the first RFQ to the signed rate contract, what actually happened?"**
   *Intent: anchor narrative — get the real procurement cycle length, number of vendors, and process.*

2. **"How many vendors did you actually get quotes from for that? How did you find them — existing empanelled list, IndiaMART, referrals, site visits, something else?"**
   *Intent: tests vendor-discovery pain and whether the vendor pool is artificially small (a strong signal for a breadth/discovery wedge).*

3. **"How did you compare the quotes — was it apples-to-apples (same spec, same brand) or did you have to normalize different units, packaging, or specs yourself? Tell me about a specific instance."**
   *Intent: tests the real pain of comparing ₹/sqft vs ₹/box vs ₹/piece across vendors — directly validates our dual-unit / normalized pricing thesis.*

4. **"Was there a case recently where a vendor's quoted price or spec didn't match what was actually delivered or installed? What happened, and what did you do about it?"**
   *Intent: surfaces provenance/verification pain at the highest-stakes point — big-volume orders, real financial exposure.*

5. **"How do you currently track vendor performance and pricing history over time — is there a system, an Excel sheet, an ERP module? Show me if you can."**
   *Intent: system-of-record check; also surfaces whether there's an existing tool we'd need to beat or integrate with (SAP Ariba, Zoho, custom ERP, nothing).*

6. **"Tell me about the last time a project got delayed or a cost overrun happened specifically because of a materials sourcing issue — wrong spec ordered, vendor ran out of stock, price spiked. What was the actual impact — time, money?"**
   *Intent: quantifies the pain in real ₹ and real days — the strongest ammunition for a future pitch, and tells us if this segment even feels pain here vs. treating it as routine.*

7. **"When a new material or brand comes on the market that you haven't worked with before, how do you find out about it and vet it — credentials, samples, site visits, BIS certification checks?"**
   *Intent: tests appetite for a discovery/vetting layer and validates the compliance/certification data thesis (BIS/GreenPro).*

8. **"Do you use any software or platform today specifically for vendor sourcing or price benchmarking — for materials specifically, not just general ERP? What, and what do you use it for?"**
   *Intent: direct WTP-via-past-behaviour + competitive landscape.*

9. **"Who signs off on a rate contract once you've negotiated it — just you, a committee, the promoter/owner? How long does that approval typically take?"**
   *Intent: identifies real buyer/decision unit for enterprise sales — often not the person you're talking to.*

10. **"Is there a budget line specifically for procurement tools/software, separate from the materials budget itself? Roughly how has that been spent in the last year?"**
    *Intent: tests whether tooling spend already exists as a line item — a much stronger WTP signal than "we'd probably pay for that."*

11. **"What's the single most time-consuming part of running a vendor/rate-contract process for finishes, specifically?"**
    *Intent: open pain surfacing for wedge validation.*

### 7. Oblique sourcing / commission-alignment probe
This segment is often the *counter-camp* — many developers/PMCs actively want transparency because kickbacks hurt *their* margins (someone else's commission is their cost). Probe gently to confirm which side they're actually on:

- *"On that last rate contract — did any of the shortlisted vendors come through a recommendation from your site engineers, architects, or designers on the project, rather than your own sourcing? How did you handle that?"*
- *"Have you ever suspected — or had it confirmed — that a design consultant on a project was steering specs toward a particular vendor for their own reasons? What did you do, if anything?"*
- *"How strict is your organization about competitive quotes — is it a hard rule (minimum 3 quotes, documented), or more relationship-driven in practice?"*

**Listen for:** procurement heads who volunteer stories about pushing back on architect-recommended vendors, or who describe rigid multi-quote policies, are strongly transparency-aligned — they are natural allies/customers for a price-comparison product. Ones who get vague or say "that's not really something I'd know about" may be in an organization where it's tolerated — note it, don't push.

### Wrap
- *"This is exactly the kind of detail I needed — thank you. Anyone else on your team, or at another developer/PMC, who runs a similar process and might be willing to talk?"*
- *"Okay if I reach out again once I have something more concrete?"*

## 4. Material Dealers / Distributors

### Screening criteria
Recruit only if **all** of:
- Owns or manages a dealership/distribution business for tiles, sanitaryware, laminates, lighting, or surfaces (not a single brand's direct factory outlet only — want someone who carries multiple brands and deals with the trade).
- Has been operating for ≥2 years (enough history to talk about how the referral/trade economics actually work).
- Has had walk-in and project/trade (architect/designer-referred) business in the **last month** — need current, not historical-only, behaviour.
- Get a mix: one dealer primarily serving retail/homeowners, one primarily serving the design trade (architects/designers bring most business).

### Warm-up
- "Tell me about the business — what brands/categories do you carry, how long have you been running it, roughly how big is the showroom/team?"
- "Rough split of your business — walk-in retail customers vs. trade/project business through designers, architects, or contractors?"

### Core questions

1. **"Walk me through the last big trade/project order you closed — who brought it to you, how did the conversation start, what happened from first contact to final delivery?"**
   *Intent: anchor narrative on the trade side of the business.*

2. **"How do designers or architects typically find you in the first place — do they call you cold, come through a referral, find you online, or something else? Tell me about the most recent new trade contact you got."**
   *Intent: tests dealer discoverability pain — a potential wedge if dealers can't be found/compared easily either.*

3. **"When a designer or architect calls asking about a specific product for a client, how do you usually give them a price — is there a standard 'trade price' that's different from what a walk-in customer would pay?"**
   *Intent: surfaces the existence and structure of trade/dealer pricing tiers — foundational to the commission economy question, asked as normal trade practice, not accusation.*

4. **"How is that trade price usually settled with the designer or architect — do they take a cut on top before quoting the client, or does the client pay you directly and the difference gets handled separately?"**
   *Intent: this is the most direct (but still process-framed, not accusatory) question in the whole interview set — dealers are usually far less defensive about this than the designers/architects themselves, since it's just "how trade discounts work" from their side.*

5. **"Do you track which designers/architects send you the most business? Roughly how much of your revenue last year came through the top handful of them?"**
   *Intent: quantifies referral concentration — tells us if a small number of relationships dominate, which matters for any two-sided platform strategy.*

6. **"Tell me about a time a customer — retail or trade — pushed back hard on your price because they'd seen something cheaper elsewhere, online or at another dealer. What did you do?"**
   *Intent: tests price transparency pressure the dealer already faces — a proxy for how disruptive real price-comparison would be to their current model.*

7. **"How do you currently market or list your inventory and pricing — website, IndiaMART, WhatsApp catalog, Instagram, none of the above? What's worked, what hasn't?"**
   *Intent: discovery-channel and existing spend on visibility — sets up the WTP question.*

8. **"Do you pay for any listing, lead-generation, or advertising service today — IndiaMART premium, Google ads, Instagram promotion, anything? What do you pay, and has it actually brought you business you can point to?"**
   *Intent: direct WTP-via-past-behaviour for a potential dealer-facing product (visibility/leads).*

9. **"Tell me about the last time you lost a sale specifically because a customer or trade contact couldn't quickly confirm your price, stock, or specs, and went elsewhere instead."**
   *Intent: quantifies the cost of the current opacity from the seller's side — often invisible to the dealer until asked directly.*

10. **"When a new brand or product line comes to you wanting to be stocked, how do you evaluate whether to carry it? Has a brand ever asked you to help them get in front of more architects/designers?"**
    *Intent: probes the supply side of our eventual "brands pay for placement" model — do dealers already get pitched something like this by brands?*

### 7. Oblique sourcing / commission-alignment probe
For dealers, the framing can be slightly more direct because trade discounts are a normal, openly discussed part of their business — but still avoid moralizing language ("kickback," "commission," "under the table"):

- *"Just so I understand the mechanics — when the trade discount goes to the designer instead of the end client, does the client generally know that's happening, in your experience?"*
- *"Has a client ever asked you directly what the 'real' price was, separate from what their designer quoted them? What did you do?"*
- *"Some dealers I've talked to say they'd actually prefer if pricing were more transparent across the market — others say the trade relationships depend on it staying flexible/negotiated. Which way do you lean, and why?"*

**Listen for:** dealers who describe the client-facing opacity as *useful to them* (protects margins, keeps designer relationships loyal) are in the threatened camp for any transparency product aimed at end clients — but may be *customers* for a B2B lead-gen/visibility product instead. Dealers who complain about being undercut by online listings or WhatsApp price-shopping are more transparency-neutral and may see cross-supplier listing as a way to compete on service/stock rather than hidden pricing.

### Wrap
- *"This has been genuinely useful — thanks for being candid. Any other dealers, or a brand rep you deal with, who'd be worth talking to?"*
- *"Mind if I follow up as this develops — might have something concrete to show you."*

## 5. Brand Marketing / Sales Managers (Manufacturer Side)

### Screening criteria
Recruit only if **all** of:
- Works in marketing, sales, or channel/trade management at a materials manufacturer or brand (tiles, laminates, sanitaryware, lighting, surfaces) — India market responsibility, not purely export.
- Personally involved in decisions about dealer network, architect/designer outreach, or digital listing/marketing spend.
- Has run or been involved in an architect/designer-facing marketing activity (event, gifting program, digital campaign, dealer training) in the **last 6 months**.
- Mix of a large/established brand and a smaller/challenger brand if possible — their channel economics differ a lot.

### Warm-up
- "What's your role, and what part of the business do you cover — which product lines, which regions?"
- "How is the brand's go-to-market structured — direct dealer network, distributors, both?"

### Core questions

1. **"Tell me about the last major initiative you ran to get in front of architects or designers — an event, a gifting program, a digital push, a dealer incentive. What did you actually do, and what was the result?"**
   *Intent: anchor narrative on demand-gen spend and effort toward specifiers.*

2. **"How do you currently know which architects or designers are actually specifying your products versus just being 'aware' of the brand? Walk me through how you'd find that out for a specific project."**
   *Intent: tests whether attribution/tracking of specification exists at all today — a real gap most brands have no good answer for.*

3. **"Tell me about the last time you found out — after the fact — that a competitor's product got specified on a project you were pursuing. How did you find out, and what did you do?"**
   *Intent: surfaces competitive-intelligence pain, a potential wedge (market intelligence/analytics product for brands).*

4. **"How do your dealers currently represent your full catalog to customers — do they have all your SKUs listed somewhere digitally, or does it depend on what's physically in the showroom?"**
   *Intent: tests catalog-completeness pain at the dealer level — validates whether brands would want a digital canonical listing beyond their own website.*

5. **"What do you currently spend on IndiaMART, Google/Meta ads, or other digital listing/lead platforms? Walk me through the last renewal or budget decision for one of those — what made you keep or drop it?"**
   *Intent: direct WTP-via-past-behaviour and reveals which existing channels we'd be competing with or could partner through.*

6. **"Tell me about the last time a dealer or distributor asked you for better marketing support, co-op funds, or digital listing help. What did you actually provide?"**
   *Intent: tests appetite and precedent for the eventual "brands pay for placement" model — has this ask already existed in some form?*

7. **"How do you currently track or benchmark your pricing against competitors' in the market — MRP, dealer price, project quotes? Tell me about the last time you needed that information and how you got it."**
   *Intent: tests appetite for market pricing intelligence — a plausible paid-data-product wedge for brands specifically.*

8. **"Have you ever had a dealer or a project quote your product at a price that surprised you — too high, too low, inconsistent with your MRP? What happened?"**
   *Intent: surfaces channel-pricing-control pain, related to trust/provenance thesis from the brand's side.*

9. **"What's the budget approval process for a new marketing/data tool or platform at your company — who signs off, what's the typical size of spend that doesn't need a lot of approval?"**
   *Intent: identifies real buyer and deal-size ceiling for a future brand-facing product (Phase F "the flip").*

10. **"If you think about where architects and designers actually go today to discover new products or verify specs, where do you think that is — and how do you know?"**
    *Intent: tests the brand's own model of the specifier's discovery journey — useful both as signal and as a cross-check against what designers/architects actually told us.*

### 7. Oblique sourcing / commission-alignment probe
This segment sits on the *supply* side of the commission economy — brands often know (or suspect) that architect/dealer margins exist, and some brands informally fund architect relationships (gifting, incentive trips, "loyalty" programs). Probe without asking "do you fund kickbacks":

- *"Do you run any kind of loyalty, incentive, or recognition program specifically for architects or designers who specify your products? Tell me about the last one and how it worked."*
- *"How do you think about the relationship between your dealer's trade discount and whether an architect or designer ends up recommending your brand over a competitor's? Has that ever come up explicitly in a business conversation?"*
- *"If a designer asked you directly for a better trade price because they were specifying your brand for a client, how would that conversation typically go?"*

**Listen for:** brands that have a formal "specifier loyalty" or incentive program are implicitly acknowledging and operating within the commission economy — they would likely see a transparency-first platform as a *threat to channel control*, but might still pay for demand-gen/data if it doesn't disrupt their existing dealer economics. Brands that emphasize "we compete on product/price, not relationships" are more aligned with a transparent comparison product and are better first design partners.

### Wrap
- *"Really helpful — thank you. Is there anyone else at the company, or at another brand, who handles channel or digital strategy that I should talk to?"*
- *"Would it be all right to check back in as this develops?"*

## 6. Cheat Sheet — Signals to Listen For

One page. Use this **during** or immediately after each interview to score the interviewee, not as a script.

| What you hear | Camp: Transparency-aligned vs. Threatened | Wedge preference this implies | WTP signal strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Client pays the dealer directly, we just bill a design fee" | **Transparency-aligned** | Spec/discovery tool, price comparison for their own use | Weak-moderate (fee-only firms have tighter tool budgets) |
| "We give the client a landed price that includes everything" / can't/won't say who the invoice is addressed to | **Threatened** | Would want a tool that *doesn't* expose their markup to clients — e.g. an internal sourcing tool, not a client-facing comparison | Moderate but conditional — needs a private, not public, comparison view |

| Dealer: "trade discount goes to whoever refers the business, that's just how it works" | Dealer is **structurally embedded** in the threatened-side economy (supplies the margin) | Dealer-side product should be visibility/leads, never client-facing price transparency | Moderate — dealers already spend on IndiaMART/ads, comparable budget exists |
| Dealer: "we lose sales when people find a lower price on WhatsApp/online and we can't match fast enough" | **Transparency-neutral-to-aligned** (wants to compete on speed/stock, not opacity) | Real-time stock/price visibility tool for dealers themselves | Moderate-strong |

| Brand: "we compete on product and price, not relationships" | **Transparency-aligned** | Good design partner for early comparison/catalog features | Strong — likely has digital marketing budget already |

**Cross-interview pattern to watch for:** if 4+ of 5 people in a segment independently name the *same* workaround tool, channel, or failure mode without being asked leading questions, that is your strongest available evidence of a real, sized problem — stronger than any single quote. Track this in a simple tally, not just individual write-ups.
